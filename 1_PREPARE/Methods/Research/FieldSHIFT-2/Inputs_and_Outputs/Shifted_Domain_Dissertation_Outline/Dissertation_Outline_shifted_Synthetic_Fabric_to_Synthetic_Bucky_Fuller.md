# PhD Dissertation Plan: Transposing the Fabric Industry and Arts into the Context of Buckminster Fuller’s Theories

## Executive Summary
This dissertation explores the innovative intersection of the fabric industry and the theories of Buckminster Fuller, proposing a transformative framework that integrates sustainable textile design with holistic, resource-efficient principles. By investigating the synergies between these two domains, this research aims to generate novel hypotheses and practical applications that can significantly impact both fields. The potential for interdisciplinary collaboration, new pedagogical approaches, and real-world innovations will be highlighted, positioning this work as a critical contribution to the future of sustainable design and cultural expression.

## Introduction
### Background of the Shifted Domain
The fabric industry has evolved significantly, moving from traditional craftsmanship to modern industrial practices. Concurrently, Buckminster Fuller’s theories emphasize systemic design solutions that address global challenges. This dissertation seeks to bridge these two domains, leveraging Fuller's principles to innovate within the fabric industry.

### Significance and Novelty of the Research
This research is significant as it addresses pressing issues such as sustainability, resource efficiency, and cultural relevance in textile design. By applying Fuller's theories to the fabric industry, this dissertation will explore new methodologies that can reshape production practices and consumer perceptions.

### Overarching Research Questions and Objectives
1. How can principles of tensegrity and geodesic design be applied to innovate textile structures?
2. In what ways can smart textiles address global challenges such as disaster relief and health monitoring?
3. What educational frameworks can foster interdisciplinary collaboration between textile design and engineering?
