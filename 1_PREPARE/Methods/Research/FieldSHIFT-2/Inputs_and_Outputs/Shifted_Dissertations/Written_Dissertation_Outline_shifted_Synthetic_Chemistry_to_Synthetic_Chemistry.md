# PhD Dissertation: Transformative Shift in Chemistry

## Executive Summary

This dissertation aims to explore the transformative potential of integrating dynamic systems thinking into the field of chemistry, creating a Shifted Domain that emphasizes interconnectedness, adaptability, and sustainability in chemical processes. By fusing traditional chemical principles with innovative methodologies, the research will address existing challenges within the field and propose new frameworks for understanding chemical reactions, catalysis, and equilibrium. The potential impact of this research spans educational reform, industrial applications, and environmental sustainability, positioning it as a groundbreaking contribution to both chemistry and interdisciplinary studies.

## Introduction

### Background of the Shifted Domain

The Shifted Domain represents a novel approach to chemistry, emphasizing the dynamic nature of chemical reactions and the interconnectedness of various processes. Traditional chemistry often isolates reactions, neglecting the broader context in which they occur. This dissertation posits that understanding chemical reactions as part of a broader network can yield new insights into chemical behavior and enhance our understanding of complex systems. The Shifted Domain invites a paradigm shift in how chemists conceptualize interactions, moving beyond linear models to embrace complexity, feedback loops, and emergent properties.

### Significance and Novelty of the Research

This research is significant because it challenges traditional views of chemistry that often treat reactions in isolation. By integrating concepts from systems theory and ecology, the dissertation seeks to provide a fresh perspective on chemical processes, leading to innovative solutions for sustainability and efficiency in chemical practices. The novelty lies in the application of dynamic systems thinking to chemical education, research, and industrial practices, fostering a holistic understanding of chemical interactions that can drive advancements in green chemistry and sustainable practices.

### Overarching Research Questions and Objectives

The dissertation is guided by the following research questions:

- How can dynamic systems thinking transform our understanding of chemical reactions and catalysis?
- What novel frameworks can be developed to model chemical processes as interconnected networks?
- How can these insights be applied to enhance sustainability in chemical practices and education?

The objectives of this research include developing a theoretical framework for the Shifted Domain, conducting empirical studies to validate the proposed models, and exploring the implications of these findings for educational reform and industrial applications.

## Theoretical Framework

### Foundational Theories from Original Domains

This section reviews foundational theories in thermodynamics, kinetics, and catalysis, providing a comprehensive overview of established models in chemistry. Thermodynamics, which governs the energy transformations in chemical reactions, has been instrumental in understanding reaction spontaneity and equilibrium. Kinetics, focusing on reaction rates and mechanisms, has elucidated the factors influencing reaction pathways. However, these traditional models often fall short in addressing the complexities of dynamic systems.

### New Theoretical Constructs Emerging from the Shift

Emerging from the Shifted Domain are new theoretical constructs such as dynamic reaction networks and equilibrium adaptation. Dynamic reaction networks conceptualize chemical reactions as interconnected pathways influenced by various factors, including temperature, pressure, and concentration. Equilibrium adaptation recognizes that equilibrium is not a static state but a dynamic process that can shift in response to changes in the environment. These constructs challenge traditional notions of chemical behavior and open new avenues for exploration.

### Proposed Integrated Theoretical Model

This dissertation proposes a comprehensive model that integrates traditional chemistry principles with dynamic systems thinking. The model visualizes chemical processes as networks of interactions, emphasizing feedback loops and emergent properties. By incorporating elements of systems theory, the proposed model aims to enhance our understanding of chemical reactions, catalysis, and equilibrium, ultimately fostering a more holistic approach to chemistry.

#### Sub-section 1: Conceptual Framework

Dynamic reaction networks are defined as systems of interconnected chemical reactions that evolve over time. Unlike traditional models that view reactions in isolation, dynamic reaction networks recognize the influence of external factors and feedback mechanisms. This section will explore the theoretical underpinnings of dynamic reaction networks, drawing on examples from complex systems in nature.

#### Sub-section 2: Experimental Validation

To test the validity of dynamic reaction networks, experiments will be designed to observe reaction pathways under varying conditions.
