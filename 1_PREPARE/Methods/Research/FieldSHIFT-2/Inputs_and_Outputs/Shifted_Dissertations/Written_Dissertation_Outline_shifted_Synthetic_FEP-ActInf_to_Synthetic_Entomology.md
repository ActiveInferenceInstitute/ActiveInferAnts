# PhD Dissertation: Transposing the Free Energy Principle into Entomology

## Executive Summary

This dissertation aims to explore the transformative potential of integrating the Free Energy Principle (FEP) from cognitive science into the field of entomology. By framing insect behavior through the lens of FEP, this research elucidates the adaptive strategies of insects, providing novel insights into their ecological roles and behaviors. The significance of this research lies in its interdisciplinary approach, enhancing our understanding of insect biology while informing practical applications in pest management and biodiversity conservation. The potential impact of this work extends to advancing educational frameworks within entomology, fostering a new generation of researchers equipped with innovative perspectives.

## Introduction

### Background of the Shifted Domain

The Free Energy Principle posits that biological organisms strive to minimize variational free energy to maintain their functional integrity. This principle, rooted in statistical mechanics and information theory, suggests that living systems are not merely passive recipients of environmental stimuli but are active agents that predict and adapt to their surroundings. Insects, as one of the most diverse and ecologically significant groups of organisms, exhibit complex behaviors and adaptive strategies that can be examined through this principle. Their remarkable ability to thrive in various environments, coupled with their intricate social structures and foraging strategies, positions insects as ideal subjects for investigating the implications of FEP.

### Significance and Novelty of the Research

This research represents a novel intersection between cognitive science and entomology, proposing that insect behavior can be understood as a process of minimizing surprise and optimizing decision-making. Traditional paradigms in entomology often focus on mechanistic explanations of behavior, neglecting the cognitive underpinnings that could provide deeper insights. By applying FEP, this dissertation challenges existing frameworks and opens new avenues for research and application, thereby enriching the field of entomology with cognitive science perspectives.

### Overarching Research Questions and Objectives

1. How can the Free Energy Principle be applied to understand insect behavior?
2. What are the implications of this application for pest management and biodiversity conservation?
3. How can educational frameworks in entomology be transformed through this interdisciplinary lens?

## Literature Review

### Historical Context of the Original Domains

#### Free Energy Principle

The Free Energy Principle has its origins in cognitive science, particularly in the works of Karl Friston, who proposed that biological systems function to minimize free energy. This principle has profound implications for understanding adaptive systems, suggesting that organisms generate predictions about their environment and update these predictions based on sensory input. This predictive coding framework emphasizes the role of inference in behavior, where organisms continuously adjust their internal models to align with external realities.

#### Insect Biology

The study of insect behavior has a rich historical context, dating back to early naturalists who documented the behaviors and life cycles of various species. Insect biology encompasses a wide range of topics, including ecology, taxonomy, and evolutionary biology. Recent advancements in technology, such as high-resolution imaging and genetic sequencing, have revolutionized our understanding of insect behavior, revealing the complexity of social structures, communication, and ecological interactions.

### Current State of Knowledge in Both Fields

#### FEP

Current research on the Free Energy Principle has expanded its application beyond human cognition to various biological systems, including animal behavior and neural dynamics. Studies have explored predictive coding and active inference in diverse contexts, providing a robust theoretical framework for understanding adaptive behavior across species.

Recent findings in insect behavior highlight the complexity of social structures, such as those seen in ant colonies and bee hives. However, there remains a gap in the application of cognitive theories, such as FEP, to these behaviors.

### Gaps and Opportunities Presented by the Shifted Domain

Despite the rich literature in both cognitive science and entomology, there is a notable lack of integration between these fields. This dissertation identifies significant opportunities for developing new models of insect behavior based on the Free Energy Principle, which could yield insights into the cognitive processes underlying insect behavior and ecology.

## Theoretical Framework

### Foundational Theories from Original Domains

The Free Energy Principle is grounded in several key concepts:

- **Variational Free Energy**: A measure of the difference between the predicted and actual states of a system. Organisms strive to minimize this energy to maintain homeostasis.
- **Markov Blankets**: A conceptual boundary that separates an organism from its environment, allowing for the exchange of information and energy.
- **Predictive Coding**: A framework suggesting that organisms generate predictions about sensory input and update these predictions based on discrepancies between expected and actual outcomes.

### New Theoretical Constructs Emerging from the Shift

This dissertation proposes a framework that combines FEP with insect behavioral ecology. By integrating cognitive principles with ecological contexts, this framework will elucidate the adaptive strategies employed by insects in response to environmental challenges.

### Proposed Integrated Theoretical Model

The proposed model illustrates the relationship between free energy minimization and insect behavior, incorporating elements of predictive coding and active inference. This model posits that insects continuously update their behavioral strategies based on environmental feedback, optimizing their actions to reduce surprise and enhance survival.
